Heading Hierarchy

This Is an H2 Section Heading

font-size: 1.35rem mobile → 1.65rem desktop | font-family: Kanit | weight: 600 (semi-bold)

The h2 is used for each major section of a blog post. It should be clearly larger than body text and distinctly different from h3 subheadings. Each section gets one h2 at the top.

This Is an H3 Subsection Heading

font-size: 1.1rem mobile → 1.2rem desktop | font-family: Kanit | weight: 500 (medium)

The h3 is used for subsections within a section. For example, if the section is "Haircut Frequency by Style," each style (Fades, Short Cuts, etc.) gets an h3. It should be noticeably smaller than h2 but still stand out from body text.

Another H3 Subsection

Notice the spacing: h3 has more margin-top (1.5rem) to create separation from the previous paragraph, and 0.75rem margin-bottom for breathing room before its content begins.

Bold, Italic, Links, and Emphasis

This paragraph contains bold text for emphasis and italic text for softer emphasis. You can also combine bold italic when something is really important.

FAQ Section

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should men get a haircut?

Most men should get a haircut every 2 to 4 weeks. Fades and tapers need maintenance every 2 to 3 weeks. Short natural cuts hold shape for 3 to 4 weeks. Longer styles can go 4 to 6 weeks.

Does cutting your hair make it grow faster?

No. Hair grows from the follicle at an average rate of about half an inch per month. Regular haircuts remove damaged ends and maintain shape, but do not change the growth rate.

How do I know when I need a haircut?

Common signs: your fade has lost its blend, your neckline looks uneven, your lineup has grown out, or your hair takes longer to style. If you notice these, you have waited too long.

What Professional Grooming Actually Looks Like

Most people think a haircut is a haircut. You sit down, the barber cuts, you leave. But professional grooming operates on a completely different level. It is a system built on consistency, documentation, and trust between the client and the barber.

The best haircuts are not accidental. They are maintained. That requires a barber who knows your hair as well as you do. — Clifton Al-Hakeem Bey

What Your Barber Tracks

A professional barber does more than remember your name. They build a profile over time:

  • Your preferred style and any variations you have tried
  • How your hair grows and in which directions
  • Problem areas like thinning, cowlicks, or uneven growth
  • Products that work for your texture and lifestyle
  • Seasonal adjustments to your routine

A Schedule That Works

The simplest way to stay sharp is to set a recurring schedule. Here is a starting point:

  1. Decide on your style and discuss maintenance expectations
  2. Book your first appointment and establish a baseline cut
  3. Return in 2 to 3 weeks for your first maintenance visit
  4. Evaluate and adjust the timing based on growth
  5. Lock in a recurring schedule that fits your calendar
Service Frequency Purpose
Full Haircut Every 2–4 weeks Shape, blend, and structure
Lineup Only Every 1–2 weeks Crisp edges between cuts
Beard Trim Every 2–3 weeks Even growth and definition

The goal is not to be at the barbershop constantly. The goal is to never let things get to a point where your barber is correcting instead of maintaining. That distinction saves you time, money, and frustration.

When you commit to a schedule, you stop thinking about when your next haircut is. It becomes automatic, and so does looking sharp.
